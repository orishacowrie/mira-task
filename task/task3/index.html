Task 3

Sensitive Conversation:
Letting Someone Go

How I would approach a termination conversation — with clarity, respect, and structure.

One of the most important management lessons I've learned is captured in a simple idea:

"Let people leave with dignity."

To me, this means being clear, respectful, and acknowledging a person's contribution — so that they can leave with their head held high.

Therefore, I would approach this situation not just as a termination, but as a structured and respectful transition, balancing business needs, risks, and human impact.

Preparation

Before the Conversation

I would first align with the HR Manager, Legal (if needed), the employee's direct manager, and IT/Security.

Key items to clarify:

  • Business rationale for the restructuring and why this role/person is no longer a fit
  • Final severance, notice period, and any outplacement support (aligned with policy/contract)
  • Access revocation plan (systems, data, devices)
  • Internal communication plan for the team (neutral and respectful)
  • Proper documentation of the decision

This ensures that the conversation is clear, consistent, and risk-aware.

Conversation Strategy

Given the potential emotional reaction, I would structure the conversation to be:

Clear & Direct

The decision is final. No ambiguity, no room for negotiation about the decision itself.

Respectful & Calm

No blame or over-explanation. The employee's dignity is protected throughout.

Contained

Not turning it into a negotiation. Clear boundaries around what is and isn't up for discussion.

Supported

Conducted with both the direct manager and HR present — the right balance of business context and employee support.

Timing & Process Design

I would pay particular attention to the timing and structure of the process, especially given the potential emotional reaction and risks.

Process Structure

  • All necessary steps prepared in advance, including access management to systems and data
  • Careful timing of the conversation — ensuring privacy, sufficient time, and minimal disruption to the team

I would also separate the decision conversation from the exit interview:

Initial Conversation

Focuses on clearly communicating the decision and next steps. The message is direct and unambiguous.

Follow-up Exit Interview

Next day, ideally with a different HR representative — allows the employee to share feedback in a less emotional state.

This helps both reduce immediate stress and collect more meaningful insights for the company.

Exit Approach

I would present a clear and compliant exit, aligned with local legislation and company policy. Where appropriate and aligned with leadership, I would explore additional support measures to ease the transition.

Support Measures

  • Flexibility in how the departure is positioned externally (e.g., allowing the employee to frame it as a voluntary move)
  • A reasonable notice period or garden leave (where feasible and low-risk), allowing time for knowledge handover and job search while remaining on payroll
  • Commitment to fair and positive references
  • Career support, depending on company practice (e.g., coaching, CV support)

The key is to provide support without creating ambiguity about the decision itself.

Companies that handle separations with dignity and financial support see significantly lower legal risks, reduced likelihood of negative public reviews, and better employer brand perception.

Risk Management

In parallel, I would ensure that company risks are controlled:

System Access

Access to sensitive systems is managed appropriately and revoked at the right moment.

Confidentiality

Confidentiality obligations are reinforced with the departing employee.

Documentation

The process is properly documented to protect both parties.

Internal Comms

Internal communication is neutral and respectful — protecting the employee's reputation.
