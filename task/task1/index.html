Task 1

Performance Review
Process Rollout

Implementation plan for rolling out a new performance review process in Notion — from design to adoption.

Assumptions

For the purpose of this task, I am working with the following assumptions:

01

This is a startup — the review process is straightforward and does not include role-specific competencies. The form covers: goals, values, achievements, development areas, next steps, and a final rating.

02

The rating directly affects bonus, promotion, and talent pipeline decisions.

03

Performance Reviews are conducted quarterly. Each cycle includes both blocks — results and values.

04

The company uses a task tracker (Jira/Asana or similar).

05

The process flow: goal-setting → self-review → manager review → meeting → calibration → final ratings → decisions.

06

Before redesigning the process, feedback was collected from employees and managers. Changes were validated with external specialists where internal expertise was lacking.

07

Managers know how to set goals. Each employee's goals reflect department goals, which derive from company goals.

08

After each cycle, a survey is conducted to collect feedback — results are used to improve the process.

09

For each value, there are 5 levels of behavioral expression with examples. The comment field is activated only when there is a discrepancy between self-review and manager review.

Validation

Before moving to validation methods, I would start by asking a few foundational questions: what problem are we solving through performance reviews? What problem do the current changes address? Does each change serve our primary goal?

Before launch

3–4 weeks before the general launch — a pilot with 10–15 people from different teams, roles, and levels. I collect feedback through an anonymous survey and a focus group. I look at specific metrics: time to complete, completeness of responses, quality of self-assessments and manager feedback. Since feedback was already collected before the redesign, the pilot is a final validation check, not starting from scratch.

After the cycle

The survey is a built-in part of every cycle. I analyze results compared to previous periods: % completed on time, average process NPS, data quality. 1–2 months later I conduct several 1:1s with managers and employees — looking at the impact of reviews on motivation and development.

Long term (1–2 years)

I track the correlation between performance review analytics trends and business and HR metrics: whether there is a connection with engagement, turnover, and productivity dynamics.

Communication and training

At least one month before launch is dedicated to communication, training, and team preparation. Timelines and deadlines run as a consistent thread through all activities.

Timeline Communication Training
T−4 weeks Live online meeting with managers, CEO, and HR Manager. Announcement across all corporate channels (email, Slack). All messages are connected to company values, business results, and personal benefits for each employee — with a specific focus on changes to the form and questions: why they changed and what this brings. —
T−2 weeks Meetings of managers and HR Generalist with their teams. Reminders across corporate channels (email, Slack, etc.). Separate live online sessions for managers and employees — different content, different focus. Notion guide + Q&A — a live document, updated as questions come in. Short Loom videos available to everyone — can be revisited at any time. Micro-learning — short tips in regular corporate newsletters throughout the launch period.
T−1 week Deadline reminders 2–3 times per week. Post-training tests with gamification elements: fast and correct completion earns small rewards, individual or team-based. This serves both as a knowledge check and as additional motivation to complete on time.
T+0 Each person receives a direct link to their form. Automated reminders are active. —

Role of the HR Manager

The HR Manager and CEO sign the main written communications — this signals that the process is important and supported at the leadership level.

The HR Manager independently runs the meeting with managers and delivers the final communication on results after the cycle closes.

All operational work sits with the HR Generalist. The HR Manager steps in for escalations.

How to improve completion rate

  • Completing reviews on time is included in managers' KPIs.
  • All key milestones are added to participants' calendars in advance.
  • Maximum automation of data loading: goals are pulled from the task tracker automatically.
  • A balance between numerical ratings and open-ended questions — the form does not overload.
  • Real-time dashboard — managers can see their team's progress.
  • Automated notifications from Notion triggered by key events.
  • Gamification: Early Birds — team and individual rewards for completing on time.
  • Forms are optimized for mobile.
  • For each value, there are 5 levels of behavioral expression with examples. The comment field is activated only when there is a discrepancy — this saves time on completion.

Notion architecture

Five interconnected databases. Data is pulled automatically via Relation and Rollup.

Where we eliminate manual work

  • Goal data is pulled automatically — the employee opens the form with goals already populated. Estimated saving: 30–40% of completion time.
  • Review cards are created automatically at the start of each cycle — HR does not manually create 70 cards.
  • The final score is calculated automatically using a formula — HR does not consolidate spreadsheets by hand.
  • Deadline and status-change notifications are sent automatically via Zapier → Slack / Email.
  • Export to Google Sheets after the cycle closes — automatically via Zapier.

Where we ensure consistency

  • A single template — everyone completes the same form with the same questions and rating scales.
  • Behavioral anchors for each value level eliminate subjectivity and inconsistencies during calibration.
  • A single formula for calculating the final rating — the result does not depend on the individual manager.
  • Statuses: Not Started → In Progress → Submitted → Manager Review → Completed.
  • Calibration is based on the same data — gaps between managers are immediately visible.

Scalability

✦ Scalable architecture

This architecture is scalable — the company can grow without changing its HR stack. As the team grows from 70 to 300+ people, only the number of cards changes, not the structure of the system. New values or adjustments to formula weights are made in one place. Notion + Zapier + Google Sheets is a stack that does not need to be replaced until the enterprise level.

Areas for process growth

When resources allow — deepen the Development section: each development area is linked to specific actions for both the employee and manager, with a deadline. At the start of the next cycle, the first question is: was the development plan completed? This closes the loop between the review and actual development. For the first cycle, this level of detail is excessive — what matters more is launching the process and achieving completion rate. The architecture already supports this expansion.

Risk map

Category Risk Mitigation
Data privacy In Notion it is difficult to restrict row-level access — an employee may accidentally see someone else's review through search. Filtered Views with personal links, or Notion Sites / Fillout for data entry without access to the main database.
Technical failure Zapier/Make breaks down — data from the task tracker or to Google Sheets does not transfer. Error alerts in Slack for the HR admin. Manual input instructions as a fallback.
Low engagement The same 60% of employees continue to miss deadlines. Gamification, mobile-first form design, hard link between review completion and bonuses or L&D budget.
Subjectivity (Bias) Managers rate based on personal preference rather than results. Calibration Sessions. Behavioral anchors. Evidence field for each rating.
Low quality of goals Goals in the task tracker are vague — data in the form becomes meaningless. Audit of goals together with managers before the first cycle launches.
Loss of trust Review results did not lead to real decisions — people stop taking the process seriously. Before launch, align with the CEO on how results connect to decisions, and communicate this clearly to the team.
